Machine Learning Mapping NO2 datasets

Keeping track of global NO2 levels over time is an important way for researchers to monitor climate change. In order to accurately measure the NO2 pollutant levels in our atmosphere, the Cohen Research Group created the (BEHR) Berkeley High-Resolution NO2 Product to generate high-resolution NO2 measurements. The WRF-CHEM climate model that they used to create BEHR corrected for spatial and seasonal variations in NO2 levels recorded by the OMI instrument onboard NASA's Aura satellite. In an effort to build on their work, I began the groundwork on a Machine Learning model that might be used to replace the computationally expensive WRF-CHEM climate model that produces these measurements. In my work with the Cohen Group, I successfully aligned 47K+ WRF-Chem pixels with OMI pixels to enable better data correlation during training for an ML model. I also created easily visualized maps of geolocation data measurements.

Natural Language Processing Neural Sequence Labeling

Part-of-speech tagging is the process of marking the part of speech for words, which can be performed by creating a Neural Sequence Labeling model. POS tagging is considered an integral part of NLP, in order to utilize the syntax of languages to better understand language semantics (the study of meaning in language). I built a POS-tagger model by implementing an LSTM and using mini-batch stochastic gradient descent on pre-trained GloVe embeddings. I was able to improve model accuracy by implementing a bi-directional LSTM with a dropout layer and loading the 500,000 most common 300-dimensional word embeddings.

Sociolinguistic Variation Study at UC Berkeley Analyzing Variation in Confirmation and Declination responses

Members of the same linguistic community have a lot of variation in how they speak and communicate the same ideas. Often this linguistic variation can be traced to certain social differences and social interactions. In this study, I explored the sociolinguistic variation in "yes" and "no" response variants among English speakers at UC Berkeley. By collecting auditory linguistic data over the span of two weeks, I looked for significant correlations between variant usage and social factors of gender, race, formality of relationship, and situational context.
